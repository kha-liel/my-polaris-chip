This is Cloud Gate, officially known as The Bean, located at the AT&T Plaza in Millenium Park, downtown Chicago. It was created by Anish Kapoor for a highly reflective, interactive, and monumental sculpture that engages with the city's skyline, sky, and lake. Its visual aspect, ispired by liquid mercury, is to distort and blend the surrounding urban environment to make viewers a part of the artwork.

This is the Statue of Liberty located on Liberty Island in New York Harbour. It was created by sculptor Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi and engineer Gustave Eiffel as a gift from France to celebrate American independence and the shared goal of liberty. Its visual aspects are full of meaning: the torch lights the way to freedom, the crown's spikes represent the seven seas and continents, and the broken chains at her feet signify the end of slavery and moving past unfair control.

This is the Golden Gate Bridge, which connects San Francisco and Martin County in California. It was created by engineer Joseph Strauss, designer Charles Ellis, and architect Irving Morrow to provide a much-needed travel an dtrade across the water. Its visual aspects were designed with both beauty and safety in mind: the famous International Orange color was chosed to help the bridge stand out in thick fog and its Art Deco style, seen in the tail towers and straight lines, was meant to make it look like a modern work of art.

This is the Gateway Arch, located in St. Louis, Missouri, along the Mississippi River. It was created by architect Eero Saarinen and engineer Hann Frederich to serve as a monument to the westward growth of the United States. Its visual aspect, a tall and sleek stainless steel curve, is meant to act as a Gateway to the West, while its unique weighted arch shape symbolizes strength and the bold spirit of people moving toward a new frontier.

This is the Space Needle, located at the Seattle Center in Seattle, Washington. It was created by hotel executive Edward Carlson and architect John Graham Jr. as the centerpiece for the 1962 World's Fair to show off America's goals for the Space Age. Its visual aspects are meant to look futuristic and thin: the top house is shaped like a flying saucer to represent space travel, while its wasp-waisted legs were inspired by an abstract sculpture of a dancer to give it a graceful and modern look.
